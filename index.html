Day 1 · March 15, 2021

Departure of Ever Given

The Ever Given leaves Port of Tanjung Pelepas, Malaysia — one of Asia's busiest transshipment hubs.

Cargo: 20,000 containers. Destination: Rotterdam, Netherlands. Estimated journey: ~25 days.

Day 2 · Strait of Malacca

The World's Busiest Strait

Over 90,000 vessels pass through the Strait of Malacca each year — second only to the English Channel in traffic density.

The Ever Given navigates it without incident.

Day 4 · Indian Ocean

Open Water

Steady. 13 knots. The ship's automated systems keep her precisely on course. Nothing to report.

1,200 kilometres of open ocean still to cross before the Arabian Sea.

Day 5 · Arabian Sea

Passing South of India

Routine. The watch officers rotate. Containers shift almost imperceptibly with the swell. Everything is on schedule.

Day 6 · Gulf of Aden

Gateway to the Red Sea

The Ever Given transits the Bab-el-Mandeb Strait — "Gate of Tears" in Arabic — the narrow passage separating Africa from the Arabian Peninsula.

Beyond it lies the Red Sea, and beyond that, Suez.

Day 7 · Red Sea

Northward Passage

1,930 kilometres of the Red Sea lie ahead. Hot, narrow, and flanked by desert on both sides.

The final leg before the canal.

Day 8 · Approaching Suez

The Point of No Return

Ships queue at the southern approach in convoy groups. There is no overtaking. No turning back.

Without this canal, the alternative is a 9,000 km detour around the Cape of Good Hope.

March 23 · 07:00 AM

Entering the Canal

The Ever Given enters from Port Suez in the south. At 400 metres, it is one of the largest vessels ever to make this transit.

Two local canal pilots board. The journey into the narrow corridor begins.

The Fragility of Everything
One ship. One sandstorm.
$400 million lost. Per hour.

The Ever Given was an accident. But those six straits on the previous screen are not accidents waiting to happen, they are constants. Every one is held open by a combination of politics, weather, and luck.

A war, a drought, a stranded ship, or a single missile can shut any one of them. And when it does, the effects don't stay at sea. They travel to factory floors, fuel stations, and kitchen stoves — in countries that never knew the strait existed.
